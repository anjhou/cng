  • Most major interstate natural gas pipelines in North America began operations between the 1950s and 2000s, while newer shale-related pipelines were commissioned after 2015.
  • Key global choke points impacting pipeline economics include the Strait of Hormuz, Turkish Straits, Suez Canal, Panama Canal, Baltic Sea corridor, and Ukraine transit routes.
  • Large operators include Gazprom, Kinder Morgan, Williams, Enterprise Products, CNPC, GAIL, Petrobras, and Sonatrach.
  • Typical natural gas transportation tariffs range from approximately $0.20–$1.50/MMBtu depending on distance, compression requirements, and regulatory structure.
  • Operational and maintenance (O&M) cost for major gas pipelines typically ranges between 1–4% of installed capital cost annually.
  • NGL pipelines generally have lower per-unit transport cost than trucking or rail, commonly ranging from $1–$5 per barrel depending on route length.
  • Russian and European pipelines experienced significant utilization declines after the Russia–Ukraine conflict, leading Europe to increase LNG imports from the U.S., Qatar, and Norway.
  • Permian Basin pipeline expansions in the U.S. were primarily driven by associated gas production growth and LNG export demand from the Gulf Coast.
  • African gas pipeline developments face security risks, financing constraints, and geopolitical instability, particularly in the Sahel and North African transit corridors.
  • Asian gas pipelines increasingly support long-term energy security strategies, especially China’s diversification away from seaborne LNG imports and India’s expansion of city gas distribution networks.
