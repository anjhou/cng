  • Major LNG export terminals typically operate between 5–80 million tons per annum (MTPA), equivalent to approximately 0.7–11 Bcf/d of natural gas throughput.
  • Large NGL and condensate export terminals commonly move between 200,000 bpd and 3 million bpd of propane, butane, ethane, and condensate streams.
  • U.S. Gulf Coast LNG cargoes primarily move toward Europe, South Korea, Japan, China, and India, especially after Europe reduced Russian pipeline gas imports.
  • Qatar’s Ras Laffan exports mainly target Asia-Pacific markets, including China, India, Japan, South Korea, and Southeast Asia.
  • Russian LNG exports from Yamal primarily use ice-class LNG carriers transporting cargoes through Arctic routes toward Europe and Asia.
  • Major LNG shipping companies operating from these ports include Nakilat, Mitsui O.S.K. Lines (MOL), Teekay LNG, BW LNG, Dynagas, and K Line.
  • Leading LPG and NGL ship owners include Dorian LPG, Avance Gas, BW LPG, Navigator Gas, and EXMAR.
  • Typical LNG marine transportation costs range from approximately $0.70–$3.50/MMBtu depending on shipping distance, vessel charter rates, Panama Canal congestion, and boil-off gas management.
  • Condensate and NGL freight rates are highly impacted by VLGC and VLCC vessel availability, weather disruptions, and strategic maritime chokepoints such as the Strait of Hormuz, Suez Canal, and Panama Canal.
  • Many LNG terminals are expanding storage tanks, liquefaction trains, and jetty capacity due to growing global gas demand, energy security concerns, and long-term Asian LNG contracting activity.

  • Most major interstate natural gas pipelines in North America began operations between the 1950s and 2000s, while newer shale-related pipelines were commissioned after 2015.
  • Key global choke points impacting pipeline economics include the Strait of Hormuz, Turkish Straits, Suez Canal, Panama Canal, Baltic Sea corridor, and Ukraine transit routes.
  • Large operators include Gazprom, Kinder Morgan, Williams, Enterprise Products, CNPC, GAIL, Petrobras, and Sonatrach.
  • Typical natural gas transportation tariffs range from approximately $0.20–$1.50/MMBtu depending on distance, compression requirements, and regulatory structure.
  • Operational and maintenance (O&M) cost for major gas pipelines typically ranges between 1–4% of installed capital cost annually.
  • NGL pipelines generally have lower per-unit transport cost than trucking or rail, commonly ranging from $1–$5 per barrel depending on route length.
  • Russian and European pipelines experienced significant utilization declines after the Russia–Ukraine conflict, leading Europe to increase LNG imports from the U.S., Qatar, and Norway.
  • Permian Basin pipeline expansions in the U.S. were primarily driven by associated gas production growth and LNG export demand from the Gulf Coast.
  • African gas pipeline developments face security risks, financing constraints, and geopolitical instability, particularly in the Sahel and North African transit corridors.
  • Asian gas pipelines increasingly support long-term energy security strategies, especially China’s diversification away from seaborne LNG imports and India’s expansion of city gas distribution networks.

  • Major LNG-exporting countries such as Qatar, United States, and Australia commonly apply a combination of ASTM, ISO, and API MPMS standards for custody transfer and product certification.
  • ExxonMobil typically references ASTM, GPA Midstream, API MPMS, and ISO standards in LNG and condensate sales agreements and terminal operations.
  • Shell commonly integrates ASTM D1945, ISO 6976, and ISO LNG sampling procedures within global LNG trading contracts and marine operations.
  • BP frequently uses API MPMS standards for hydrocarbon measurement and ASTM sulfur, density, and vapor pressure methods for condensate and LPG quality verification.
  • Saudi Aramco, QatarEnergy, and ADNOC often apply customized export specifications derived from ASTM and GPA standards for LPG and condensate exports.
  • European LNG terminals heavily utilize ISO 6976, EN standards, and ASTM gas chromatography methods for calorific value harmonization across pipeline systems.
  • Most LNG contracts specify limits for nitrogen, CO₂, H₂S, sulfur, water content, and heating value to ensure compatibility with downstream regasification systems.
  • Commercial propane under ASTM D1835 HD-5 grade generally limits propylene concentration to 5 vol% and controls sulfur and moisture levels.
  • Condensate export specifications commonly define acceptable ranges for API gravity, Reid Vapor Pressure (RVP), sulfur content, and distillation endpoints.
  • Large LNG and NGL marine terminals normally require compliance with ASTM/API analytical standards before cargo loading, customs certification, and international shipping documentation approval.

  • Density values are approximate industry-average values referenced near standard conditions of 60°F and 14.7 psia unless otherwise noted.
  • Specific gravity for liquids is generally referenced relative to water at 60°F, while gas specific gravity is referenced relative to air.
  • API gravity is calculated using the equation: API = (141.5 / Specific Gravity at 60°F) − 131.5.
  • Dynamic viscosity values are highly temperature dependent and generally decrease significantly as temperature increases.
  • Kinematic viscosity is calculated from dynamic viscosity divided by fluid density and is strongly influenced by heavy hydrocarbon content.
  • HHV (Higher Heating Value) includes latent heat recovery from water vapor condensation, while LHV (Lower Heating Value) excludes latent heat.
  • Natural gas and LNG energy content values vary depending on methane concentration, nitrogen dilution, and heavier hydrocarbon fractions.
  • LNG storage conditions are near atmospheric pressure but require cryogenic temperatures around -260°F to maintain liquid phase stability.
  • NGL streams such as propane and butane are commonly stored under moderate pressure to maintain saturation conditions at ambient temperature.
  • Condensate and crude oil properties vary substantially by reservoir origin, stabilization process, dissolved gas content, sulfur concentration, and blending practices.
