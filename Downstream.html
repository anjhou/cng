Key Hydrocarbon Trade Flow Drivers and Risks

  • Asian petrochemical and refining demand growth continues driving the largest global trade flows for LPG, naphtha, crude oil, and middle distillates.
  • U.S. shale production growth has structurally transformed the U.S. into a major exporter of ethane, propane, gasoline, diesel, and light sweet crude.
  • Middle Eastern export dominance remains critical for medium and sour crude supply to Asia and Europe, sustaining global refinery feedstock balances.
  • Russia sanctions and trade rerouting continue reshaping crude and refined product flows toward Asia, increasing tanker ton-mile demand and freight volatility.
  • Geopolitical risks in the Strait of Hormuz, Red Sea, and Black Sea remain major threats to crude, marine fuel, and distillate supply chains.
  • Capital investments in export terminals, VLGC fleets, pipelines, refinery upgrades, and marine bunkering infrastructure continue expanding global hydrocarbon trade capability.
  • Environmental regulations, including IMO sulfur rules, carbon intensity targets, and renewable fuel mandates, continue reshaping fuel trade economics and refinery operations.
  • Trade imbalances between North American supply growth and Asian consumption growth continue driving expansion in long-haul hydrocarbon shipping routes.
  • Logistical bottlenecks, including Panama Canal draft restrictions, port congestion, limited pipeline takeaway capacity, and tanker shortages, periodically disrupt trade flows.
  • Refinery closures in Europe and slower refining investment globally continue increasing dependency on imported gasoline, diesel, jet fuel, and blending components.
