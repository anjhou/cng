Physical and Thermodynamic Properties of H2, LNG, NGLs, Condensate, and Crude Oil

  • Notes 1: Density values are approximate industry-average values referenced near standard conditions of 60°F and 14.7 psia unless otherwise noted.
  • Notes 2: Specific gravity for liquids is generally referenced relative to water at 60°F, while gas specific gravity is referenced relative to air.
  • Notes 3: API gravity is calculated using the equation: API = (141.5 / Specific Gravity at 60°F) − 131.5.
  • Notes 4: Dynamic viscosity values are highly temperature dependent and generally decrease significantly as temperature increases.
  • Notes 5: Kinematic viscosity is calculated from dynamic viscosity divided by fluid density and is strongly influenced by heavy hydrocarbon content.
  • Notes 6: HHV (Higher Heating Value) includes latent heat recovery from water vapor condensation, while LHV (Lower Heating Value) excludes latent heat.
  • Notes 7: Natural gas and LNG energy content values vary depending on methane concentration, nitrogen dilution, and heavier hydrocarbon fractions.
  • Notes 8: LNG storage conditions are near atmospheric pressure but require cryogenic temperatures around -260°F to maintain liquid phase stability.
  • Notes 9: NGL streams such as propane and butane are commonly stored under moderate pressure to maintain saturation conditions at ambient temperature.
  • Notes 10: Condensate and crude oil properties vary substantially by reservoir origin, stabilization process, dissolved gas content, sulfur concentration, and blending practices.
